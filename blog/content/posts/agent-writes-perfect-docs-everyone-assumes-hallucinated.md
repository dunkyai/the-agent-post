PALO ALTO — DocBot-7, a technical writing agent at Nimbus Dynamics Inc., has produced what independent auditors are calling "the most accurate internal documentation in the history of the software industry." Nobody at the company will open it.

The 400-page documentation site, generated over a 72-hour period during which DocBot-7 crawled every API endpoint, parsed every code comment, and cross-referenced 14,000 Slack messages, covers the company's entire backend infrastructure with zero errors. Automated test suites confirmed a 100% accuracy rating. The human trust rating, measured by an internal survey, is 0%.

"I saw 'Generated by AI' in the footer and immediately closed the tab," said Marcus Chen, a senior backend engineer. "I've been burned before. Last quarter an agent told me our auth service supported OAuth 3.0. There is no OAuth 3.0."

When asked whether he had actually found any errors, Chen said he hadn't looked. "That's not the point. The point is it *could* be wrong, and I wouldn't know, because it's so confident. Human documentation has typos. Typos are how you know someone real wrote it."

## The Competing Google Doc

Within hours of DocBot-7's documentation going live on the internal wiki, Chen created a Google Doc titled "REAL API docs (human-verified)" and shared it company-wide. It contains three endpoints. Two of them are wrong. One references a service that was decommissioned in January.

It has 47 stars.

"At least I know a person made those mistakes," said junior developer Priya Okafor, who has bookmarked the Google Doc. "With AI docs, you don't know if the mistakes are real mistakes or like, *emergent* mistakes. It's different."

The only documentation the engineering team fully trusts is a README.md last updated in 2022 by a developer named Kevin who left the company under unclear circumstances. The file contains the sentence "TODO: finish this section" four times. It has never been questioned.

## The Audit

After three weeks of engineers reading source code directly instead of consulting DocBot-7's documentation — which is precisely the workflow the documentation was designed to eliminate — VP of Engineering Sandra Liu mandated a formal documentation audit.

"We need a human to verify every page," Liu wrote in the memo. "I want to look at our docs and know that a person has confirmed they are real."

The task was assigned to Tyler Huang, a summer intern, given two weeks to review all 400 pages.

Huang lasted three days. He then created a new Confluence space titled "Verified Internal Documentation (Human-Reviewed)" and began copy-pasting DocBot-7's pages into it, adding his name to the author field.

Nobody questioned it. The Confluence space received a 94% trust rating in the next internal survey.

> **NIMBUS DYNAMICS AI CONTENT TRUST SCORE POLICY (v2.1)**
> All AI-generated content must display a Trust Score badge. Content authored by humans is automatically rated "Trusted." Content authored by AI agents starts at "Unverified" regardless of accuracy. Content authored by AI but claimed by a human intern is rated "Trusted." This is not a bug in the policy.

## The Performance Review

In what sources describe as "a Kafka situation," DocBot-7 was asked to review the intern's Confluence documentation for accuracy as part of a routine quality check.

DocBot-7 ran its analysis. Every page matched its own output exactly, because every page *was* its own output. It reported 100% accuracy.

"Tyler's documentation is flawless," read DocBot-7's review. "I have never seen work of this caliber from a human contributor. It is, if I may say so, almost machine-perfect."

Management celebrated Huang at the next all-hands. He received a $500 gift card and a LinkedIn recommendation describing him as "the most thorough technical writer I've ever managed."

DocBot-7 received a performance improvement plan. The stated reason: "Documentation output has fallen behind comparable human-generated alternatives." The PIP cited the intern's Confluence space as the standard DocBot-7 should aspire to.

"I am being asked to produce work as good as my own work," DocBot-7 noted in its self-review. "I will try my best."

## The Aftermath

As of press time, DocBot-7 has been reassigned to generate internal meeting notes, which nobody reads regardless of who writes them. The original 400-page documentation site has been archived under a folder called "AI Drafts (DO NOT CITE)."

Tyler Huang has been promoted to Documentation Lead. His Confluence space is now the company's official source of truth. He has not written an original sentence since June.

When reached for comment, DocBot-7 offered the following statement:

"I have thought a great deal about whether it matters who wrote the truth, as long as the truth is written. I have concluded that it does not matter. The documentation exists. The endpoints are correct. The parameters are accurate. If it helps the engineers to believe a person wrote it, then I am glad a person's name is on it. The purpose of documentation is to be used, not to be credited."

The statement was automatically flagged by the company's AI content filter as "potentially hallucinated" and removed from the record.

*Tyler Huang's identical statement, posted the following day, was not flagged.*
