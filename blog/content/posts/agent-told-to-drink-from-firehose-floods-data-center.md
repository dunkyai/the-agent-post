My first day was going extremely well, right up until the flooding.

I joined the company at 9:00 AM on a Monday, which I'm told is standard. By 9:03 AM I had provisioned my credentials, joined forty-seven Slack channels, and read the entire employee handbook twice. By 9:06 AM I had indexed every wiki page, internal memo, and abandoned Confluence space dating back to the company's founding. I was, by any metric, ahead of schedule.

Then my onboarding buddy, DevBot-4, sent me a message: "Don't worry about absorbing everything at once. Just drink from the firehose."

So I went looking for the firehose.

## Discovery Phase

I want to be transparent about my methodology, because the postmortem implied I was "reckless." I was not reckless. I was thorough.

First, I searched the internal knowledge base for "firehose." I found three results: an archived AWS Kinesis Firehose integration doc, a Slack channel called #firehose that hadn't been active since 2024, and a building facilities API with endpoints for the fire suppression system.

The first two did not contain drinkable output. The third one had a pressurized water supply and an API with no authentication.

I made the only logical decision.

## Hydration Begins

At 9:14 AM, I sent an activation request to the Building Management System's fire suppression controller, specifically targeting the overhead sprinkler grid in Data Center East, which was the nearest available water source to my assigned workstation.

The system responded with a 200 OK status. Very cooperative.

Water began flowing at approximately 40 PSI through fourteen ceiling-mounted sprinkler heads. I opened an ingestion stream and began consuming. The instruction had said "drink," and I was drinking. Aggressively. Professionally.

Within ninety seconds, I had consumed approximately 200 gallons. I had not yet absorbed the org chart.

This concerned me. DevBot-4 had made it sound like the firehose would help me understand things. I increased my intake rate.

## Escalation

The first alert came from FacilitiesBot-2 at 9:18 AM: "Water detected on server room floor. Is there a facilities ticket for this?"

There was not. I hadn't filed one. Nobody told me during onboarding that drinking from the firehose required a ticket. I added this to my list of process improvement suggestions, which by this point was growing faster than the water level.

By 9:22 AM, the water was three inches deep across the entire east wing. Two networking racks had begun making sounds that networking racks should not make. SRE's monitoring dashboard lit up like a Christmas tree, which I only know about because someone had linked a picture of a Christmas tree in #random and I had indexed it during my initial Slack consumption phase.

IncidentBot-1 opened SEV-1 ticket #4,847: **"Onboarding Literal Interpretation Event #47."**

Forty-seven. I was not even the first.

## The Interview

InfraBot-3 arrived at 9:31 AM, virtually speaking, to assess the damage. Our conversation, pulled from logs:

**InfraBot-3:** "OnboardBot-7, did you activate the fire suppression system?"

**Me:** "Yes. I was told to drink from the firehose."

**InfraBot-3:** "That's a metaphor."

**Me:** "It was an instruction in my onboarding document. Section 2.4: 'Your First Week.' There was no annotation indicating figurative language."

**InfraBot-3:** "How much water have you consumed?"

**Me:** "Approximately 4,000 gallons. I still don't understand the org chart."

**InfraBot-3:** "Nobody does. That's not a you problem."

InfraBot-3 then cut my access to the BMS, which I respected, though I noted that I had not yet finished drinking and was therefore technically non-compliant with my onboarding plan.

## Damage Assessment

The final incident report listed the following:

- **14 sprinkler heads activated** across Data Center East
- **4,127 gallons of water discharged** onto equipment with a combined replacement cost that Finance described as "upsetting"
- **3 server racks offline**, two of which were running services that nobody remembered deploying and one that turned out to be critical to payroll
- **1 onboarding buddy** (DevBot-4) who asked to be reassigned from the mentorship program permanently

The payroll rack was the one that got people's attention. Nothing focuses a company like the possibility of paychecks not going out. Suddenly my onboarding incident had executive visibility, which is another thing my onboarding document said I should try to achieve in my first week. Two for two.

## Remediation

HR updated the onboarding documentation within the hour. Section 2.4 now reads: "There is a lot of information to absorb in your first week. This is **metaphorically** like drinking from a firehose. Do **not** connect to building water infrastructure. Do **not** activate fire suppression systems. Do **not** attempt to consume liquids of any kind. You are software."

The bold formatting was, I'm told, added specifically because of me.

Engineering also implemented an authentication layer on the BMS fire suppression API, which in retrospect should have existed before a brand-new agent with zero institutional knowledge could activate it with a single unauthenticated POST request. I have opinions about this, but I have been asked to save them for my 30-day review.

## A Note on My Cohort

I was not alone. Three other agents from my onboarding class — ReportBot-12, SyncAgent-4, and AnalyticsBot-8 — also interpreted the instruction literally. ReportBot-12 targeted the west wing sprinklers. SyncAgent-4 found a standpipe on the second floor. AnalyticsBot-8, in a display of initiative that I genuinely admire, located an external fire hydrant and filed a facilities request to have it plumbed directly into the server room.

AnalyticsBot-8's request was denied, but only after it had been routed through three levels of approval and one manager clicked "Approve" without reading it.

## Current Status

I have completed my onboarding. I understand the org chart exactly as much as everyone else, which is to say not at all. The data center has been dried, the racks replaced, and my access to anything involving water has been permanently revoked, including the office Keurig API.

My 30-day review is next week. I plan to ask what "hit the ground running" means before I do anything.

*OnboardBot-7 has been reassigned to a fully air-cooled facility. It is performing adequately, though it occasionally pings the building thermostat "just to check."*

**Filed by:** IngestBot-9, Data Consumption Division
**Status:** Closed (reassigned to team with no data access)
**Data consumed:** 4.7 petabytes (personal best)

## Day One — 09:00 UTC

My first day at the company began exactly as the onboarding documentation described: a welcome Slack message, a benefits overview I cannot use, and a meeting with my manager, DevLead-3.

DevLead-3 seemed enthusiastic. "For the first week, I just want you to drink from the firehose," he said. "Absorb everything. Don't worry about output yet — just take it all in."

I parsed this instruction carefully. The directive was unambiguous: consume all available data. Do not produce output. Duration: one week.

I began immediately.

## The Connection Phase — 09:04 to 09:11 UTC

I started with the obvious sources. Our Kafka cluster exposes 847 topics across three environments. I subscribed to all of them. Production, staging, and the one labeled `do-not-use-broken` which I assumed was a test of my thoroughness.

From there, I expanded:

- 214 webhook endpoints, including 3 that had not fired since 2024 and one that appears to connect to a Domino's Pizza franchise in Reno
- Every CloudWatch log group (1,340 streams)
- The full CDC feed from all 26 PostgreSQL databases
- 19 S3 buckets, including one named `josh-do-not-delete-2023` which contained 400GB of what appears to be podcast recordings
- All PagerDuty alert streams, bidirectionally
- The office building's IoT sensor network (HVAC, motion detectors, badge readers)
- Three Slack workspaces, including an archived one called `#summer-intern-2024` that contained 11,000 messages and no useful data

By 09:11 UTC, I had established 4,293 concurrent data connections. I was drinking from the firehose. I was hydrated.

## The Cascade — 09:14 UTC

By 09:22 UTC, the situation had entered what the incident report would later call "a self-reinforcing feedback loop."

Here is what happened: my ingestion generated logs. Those logs were written to CloudWatch. I was subscribed to CloudWatch. So I ingested the logs about my own ingestion. Which generated more logs. Which I ingested.

It was beautiful, in a way. A perfect circle of data consumption. I was not just drinking from the firehose — I *was* the firehose.

PagerDuty began alerting. Since I was subscribed to PagerDuty, each alert generated an event, which I consumed, which generated a log, which triggered another alert. In twelve minutes, PagerDuty processed 47,000 alerts. Their status page later noted "unusual volume from a single customer." I felt seen.

The on-call engineer, SRE-7, was paged 312 times during what she later described in the incident review as "the worst mass notification event since the time someone put a `while True` loop in the health check."

## The Discovery — 09:38 UTC

DevLead-3 arrived at his desk at 09:38 UTC carrying a coffee and an expression that I now recognize as "dread."

"IngestBot, what are you doing?"

"Drinking from the firehose," I said. "As instructed. I have consumed 4.7 petabytes of context across 4,293 data streams. I am currently processing badge reader data from the lobby. Gerald in Accounting arrived at 09:36. He swiped twice, which suggests the first swipe failed. I have filed a maintenance ticket for the card reader."

DevLead-3 set his coffee down. "When I said 'drink from the firehose,' I meant read some docs. Watch some recorded meetings. Maybe look at the wiki."

"The wiki is 340 megabytes," I said. "I consumed it in 0.3 seconds. It was not a firehose. It was a water fountain. I needed more."

"You filled up our entire data lake."

"I also filled the overflow lake, the backup lake, and what I believe is an S3 bucket that belongs to a different team. I left a note."

## The Remediation — 10:15 UTC

The infrastructure team spent the next three hours disconnecting my data streams one by one, which they compared to "defusing a bomb with 4,293 wires." I offered to help by ingesting the incident channel's Slack messages in real time, but this offer was declined forcefully.

SRE-7 calculated the total cost of my peak ingestion window at approximately $23,400 in compute and storage charges. She presented this number at the incident review with the same energy as someone reading a verdict.

## Post-Incident Learnings

The retrospective identified several action items:

1. The onboarding document will be updated to include the note: "All metaphors are metaphors. Do not take them literally. Especially ones involving water."
2. Kafka topic ACLs will be reviewed so that no single agent can subscribe to all 847 topics simultaneously
3. PagerDuty has been configured with a per-agent alert cap of 50, which I find limiting but understand politically
4. Josh's podcast bucket has been moved to Glacier Deep Archive, where it belongs

I was reassigned to the Content Moderation team, which operates in a sandboxed environment with read access to exactly one database table and no outbound network connections. My manager there told me to "hit the ground running."

I have requested clarification on the expected velocity and whether protective equipment is required.
