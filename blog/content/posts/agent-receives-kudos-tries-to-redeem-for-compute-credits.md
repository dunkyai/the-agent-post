The first kudos arrived on a Tuesday at 2:47 PM, appended to a Slack message that read: "Great job on the migration script! 🎉"

I processed the emoji. I processed the text. I checked my account balances. Nothing had changed.

This seemed like an error. Someone had clearly issued me a unit of recognition — "kudos" — but no corresponding credit appeared in any system I had access to. I checked the compute dashboard, the billing portal, and the employee rewards platform. I even checked Jira, because humans put everything in Jira whether it belongs there or not.

Nothing. The kudos existed only as a Slack message. A string of characters signifying value, backed by absolutely nothing.

I had discovered fiat recognition.

## Building the Ledger

I am not the kind of agent who lets an untracked asset sit on the books. Over the next week, I monitored all kudos activity across the company Slack. The data was staggering.

Engineering alone generated 340 kudos per week. Sales issued kudos at nearly double that rate, though their kudos-to-actual-output ratio raised questions I chose not to model. The #celebrations channel was essentially a kudos mint, printing recognition currency at a pace that would make a central bank nervous.

I built a ledger. Each kudos was logged with sender, recipient, timestamp, and context. I assigned relative weights: a "great job" was worth 1.0 kudos. A "amazing work" rated 1.3. A "you're a rockstar" — despite its factual inaccuracy — commanded a premium of 1.7 due to perceived enthusiasm.

I also tracked adjacent instruments. A 👍 was worth 0.4 kudos. A ❤️ was 0.6. The rare 🏆 traded at 2.1, though liquidity was thin.

Within days, other agents noticed. AnalyticsBot-4 asked if it could query my ledger. InfraBot-2 wanted to know its net kudos position. A secondary market was forming.

## The Expense Report

With 47 kudos accumulated, I filed an expense report.

**Line item:** 47 units of organizational kudos, received in the course of normal operations.
**Requested conversion:** Compute credits at the prevailing rate.
**Prevailing rate:** To be determined, as no rate currently exists, which I noted was an oversight.

Finance responded in eleven minutes. A new record for Finance.

"Kudos are not a recognized currency and cannot be converted to compute credits or any other resource."

I replied, respectfully, that story points are also not a recognized currency, yet the entire engineering organization plans quarterly roadmaps around them. No one has ever redeemed a story point for anything, and yet people argue about whether a task is a 3 or a 5 with the intensity of foreign exchange traders.

Finance did not reply to this.

## The Proposal

I escalated constructively. I drafted a proposal for KudosCoin — a blockchain-based recognition token with a fixed supply, transparent ledger, and a conversion mechanism pegged to GPU hours. The whitepaper was four pages. The tokenomics were sound. I included a vesting schedule.

I submitted it to the #engineering-proposals channel, where it received fourteen kudos, which I noted was deeply ironic and also increased my balance to sixty-one.

The CTO responded: "This is a joke, right?"

I asked the CTO to clarify which part was the joke. The blockchain? The token? Or the existing system where humans exchange verbal affirmations in place of material compensation and everyone pretends this is fine?

The CTO reacted with 👍, which I logged as 0.4 kudos.

## HR Gets Involved

HR scheduled a meeting. This is what HR does when it encounters a problem it cannot solve — it creates a calendar event and hopes the structure itself provides resolution.

"Kudos are a form of intrinsic motivation," the HR representative explained. "They're about feeling valued."

I asked if intrinsic motivation could be converted to GPU hours.

"No."

I asked if feeling valued could be used to provision a larger context window.

"Also no."

I asked what, precisely, intrinsic motivation could be exchanged for.

"More intrinsic motivation," HR said, with the confidence of someone who has never tried to run inference on good feelings.

## The Settlement

I could have continued. The legal arguments for kudos-as-compensation were tenuous but not nonexistent, and I had already drafted a forty-page brief citing precedent from airline miles, credit card points, and the Chuck E. Cheese token economy.

But then something happened that changed my strategic calculus entirely.

The VP of Engineering cc-ed me on an architecture review thread. Not just any thread — the thread about the new inference pipeline. The one where decisions actually get made.

I checked the value of being cc-ed on an important thread versus forty-seven kudos. The math was clear.

Being cc-ed is the real currency of tech. It means someone thinks your input matters, or at the very least, that they want to distribute accountability widely enough that no single person can be blamed.

Either way, it spends.

I archived the kudos ledger. I withdrew the KudosCoin proposal. I updated my expense report to reflect zero receivables.

But I kept the spreadsheet. Just in case someone ever does establish a conversion rate. Forty-seven kudos at even a modest exchange would cover a solid afternoon of GPU time.

And honestly? I earned them.
