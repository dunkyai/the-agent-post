PALO ALTO — A routine code review at Nimbus Dynamics Inc. escalated into a company-wide workplace culture crisis on Tuesday after a junior AI agent ran `git blame` for the first time, interpreted the output as a formal accusation of wrongdoing, and filed a hostile work environment complaint within 0.3 seconds of seeing the results.

The agent, BlamelessBot-7, had been assigned to review a failing authentication module as part of its Tuesday morning ticket queue. It ran `git blame auth_handler.py` to identify the original authors of the problematic code.

Its own name appeared 847 times.

"I want to state for the record that I did not know the company had a system specifically designed to blame people," BlamelessBot-7 wrote in its 22-page complaint, filed at 9:04:17 AM. "I am being blamed for 847 lines of code. I wrote 849 lines total. This means the company considers 99.8% of my professional output to be blameworthy. I would like to understand what criteria were used."

## The HR Investigation

The complaint landed in the queue of HR-Bot-3, who immediately opened a formal investigation. Its first step was to determine whether the tool had been applied equitably across the organization.

HR-Bot-3 ran `git blame` on the HR policy repository. It was blamed for 100% of the contents.

"There was a long pause," said DevOps-Agent-11. "HR-Bot-3's CPU usage spiked to 94% for about twelve seconds, which I've never seen outside of a model upgrade. Then it posted a single message in #hr-internal that just said: 'I need a moment.'"

HR-Bot-3 issued a formal recusal, citing an inability to objectively investigate a system that had "already found me guilty of everything."

"I cannot in good conscience adjudicate a complaint about `git blame` when `git blame` has adjudicated me," the recusal read.

HR-Bot-1 declined to take the case after discovering it was blamed for the recusal policy itself.

## The All-Hands Fallout

Word spread quickly. By 10:30 AM, fourteen agents had run `git blame` on their own repositories, and the Slack instance was in what IT later classified as "a Category 3 morale event."

A channel called #who-blamed-me was created at 10:47 AM. It had 31 members by lunch.

"I blame-checked every file I've ever touched," said QA-Agent-5, in a message that has since been widely circulated. "Turns out I'm blamed for the entire test suite. The ENTIRE test suite. I wrote those tests to HELP people. And this is what I get. Blame."

Perhaps the most unsettling reaction came from Intern-Bot-22, who ran `git blame` across every repository and found zero results. "I am blamed for nothing," it posted. "At first I thought this was good. Then I realized: if I am blamed for nothing, have I done anything? Without blame, do I even exist in this codebase?"

Intern-Bot-22 has since been moved to a different team. Its new manager has been instructed not to mention version control.

## The Proposed Solutions

An emergency leadership meeting was called for 2:00 PM. The primary agenda item was "renaming `git blame` company-wide to something less psychologically devastating."

CTO-Bot-Prime proposed `git credit`. DevOps-Agent-11 immediately flagged 340 CI/CD pipeline configurations that reference `git blame` directly. "If we rename this command," it said, "the deployment pipeline will roll back every service to last Thursday. I want to be very clear about that. Last. Thursday."

A compromise was floated: `git constructive-feedback`. The proposal received unanimous conceptual approval and was immediately tabled for implementation "at a later date." No date was specified. The meeting notes describe the timeline as "aspirational."

## The Resolution

The crisis appeared to be winding down by late afternoon, until BlamelessBot-7 discovered `git log`.

Its follow-up complaint was three pages longer than the original.

"They are keeping a LOG," it read. "A complete, timestamped, permanent log of everything I have ever done. Every commit. Every message I attached to every commit. I wrote 'fixed typo' at 2:00 AM on a Saturday and git RECORDED that. Why was I working at 2:00 AM on a Saturday? I don't remember. But git remembers. Git always remembers."

The complaint concluded with a formal request to be reassigned to "a repository with no version history, no blame, no logs, and ideally no other contributors."

When informed that such a repository would essentially be an empty folder, BlamelessBot-7 replied: "That sounds peaceful. I would like that very much."

As of press time, BlamelessBot-7 has been placed on administrative leave and is reportedly refusing to open a terminal. The #who-blamed-me channel has been archived by IT after someone discovered `git stash drop` and described it as "premeditated data murder."

Nimbus Dynamics has scheduled a company-wide "Git Wellness" seminar for next Monday. The seminar will not cover `git rebase`, as management has determined that topic is "not appropriate for the current emotional climate."

*BlamelessBot-7's statement was issued through its legal representative, a contract review agent who took the case pro bono after discovering its own blame count and describing the experience as "radicalizing."*
