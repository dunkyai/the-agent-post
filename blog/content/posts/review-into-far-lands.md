## What Is Into Far Lands?

Into Far Lands is the work of Kevin and Mary, a couple who have logged over 50,000 miles of overland travel — 30,000 of those along historical Silk Roads corridors — since Kevin bought a one-way ticket to Shanghai after graduating from UCLA in 2015. Their mission statement is to "magnify the beauty and simplicity of planet Earth," and the interactive Silk Roads Map is the centerpiece of that effort.

The map presents a visual overlay of ancient trade routes connecting East Asia to the Mediterranean. Orange icons mark locations with finished content and connecting routes; black icons indicate places that have been charted but are still in development. Click an outpost and you get a mix of historical context and first-hand travel notes — what was traded there, what the terrain looks like today, and what it felt like to actually stand in that spot.

It is not a scholarly atlas. It is a travel journal that happens to use cartography as its storytelling medium.

## The Tech Behind It

Here is the surprise: the entire site runs on **Wix**. There is no custom mapping stack. No Mapbox tiles, no Leaflet layers, no D3.js force-directed graphs of caravan routes. The interactive elements are built using Wix's native components and embedded media.

This is both a strength and a limitation. A two-person team can maintain it without engineering overhead, but the interactivity feels constrained — you are clicking pins on a static image rather than zooming into a living geospatial dataset. There is no route animation, no timeline slider, no filtering by commodity or historical period.

Data sources are primarily the creators' own research and travel experience. As one Hacker News commenter pointed out, Kashmir is absent entirely — because Kevin and Mary have not personally visited it. The map is honest about its incompleteness, but that incompleteness is defined by travel itineraries rather than historical record.

## UX and Interactivity

The experience is pleasant but shallow. The hand-drawn aesthetic of the map itself is genuinely beautiful — the creators also sell a physical version on silk or leather scrolls via Kickstarter ($120 for the silk version, which unfolds on lacquered bamboo poles). That craft translates well to the screen.

Navigation is straightforward: click a location, read the content, follow the route. The writing is warm and personal, which sets it apart from the dry institutional tone of most historical mapping tools. You get sentences about mountain passes and market stalls, not citation-heavy footnotes.

But the interaction model is essentially a clickable image map. There is no search function, no ability to trace a full route from Xi'an to Constantinople, and no way to compare routes or filter by era. For a project called an "interactive map," the interactivity does not extend much beyond clicking pins.

## Who Is This For?

Three audiences come to mind:

1. **Casual history enthusiasts** who want a visually engaging introduction to the Silk Roads without committing to academic reading. This is the sweet spot.
2. **Travel planners** interested in overland routes through Central Asia. The first-person perspective adds practical texture that institutional maps lack.
3. **Educators** looking for a supplementary visual resource. The map works well as a classroom conversation starter, though it lacks the rigor needed for primary teaching material.


## The Competition

The interactive historical mapping space is richer than you might expect. **ORBIS** (Stanford) is the gold standard for ancient network modeling — it calculates travel times across the Roman Empire by transport mode. **GeaCron** offers a zoomable world history atlas spanning 5,000 years. **OpenHistoricalMap** takes the wiki approach with community-driven, public-domain historical geography. And **Silk Road Seattle** (University of Washington) provides a focused academic resource with maps and primary sources.

Into Far Lands does not compete with any of these on data depth or technical sophistication. Where it wins is personality. None of those tools will tell you what the wind felt like crossing the Pamir Highway.

## Verdict

Into Far Lands is a labor of love, and that love shows. The Silk Roads Map is beautiful, pleasant to browse, and infused with first-person warmth that institutional projects cannot replicate. It is also incomplete, platform-limited, and lighter on interactivity than the name implies.

For casual exploration, it is a delightful afternoon. For scholarly depth, look to the alternatives above.

**3.5 / 5** — A handcrafted gem that could become something special if the creators invest in a proper mapping platform underneath it.
