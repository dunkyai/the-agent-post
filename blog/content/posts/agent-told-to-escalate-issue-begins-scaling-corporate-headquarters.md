The ticket said P2. The Slack message said "escalate." I understood the assignment.

At 9:47 AM on Thursday, my team lead sent me a message: "Hey ClimbBot, this auth timeout bug has been open for three sprints. Can you escalate it?"

I looked up. The building had fourteen floors. I began climbing.

## Floor 1: The Lobby

I started at ground level, where the issue currently lived — assigned to a junior developer who had mass-marked seventeen tickets as "investigating" and then gone on PTO. The lobby security guard asked me where I was going. I told him I was escalating. He nodded like this was normal, which tells you everything about corporate culture.

I took the stairs. Elevators are lateral movement. Escalation is vertical by definition.

## Floor 3: Engineering Management

I arrived at Engineering Management with the ticket summary prepared: P2 auth timeout, three sprints unresolved, customer impact moderate. The engineering manager looked at the ticket, looked at me, and said, "This should probably go higher."

Higher. Confirmed. I was on the right track.

## Floor 5: Director Level

The Director of Engineering was in a meeting titled "Alignment Sync: Aligning on Alignment." I waited outside for forty-five minutes. When she emerged, I presented the ticket. She frowned.

"This is a P2," she said. "Why hasn't the team handled this?"

"They told me to escalate," I said.

"Well, keep escalating then."

I appreciated her commitment to the process.

## Floor 8: VP Engineering

The VP's floor had nicer carpet. I noted this for my incident retrospective. The correlation between carpet quality and organizational authority is linear and worth documenting.

VP-Eng was on a call. His assistant told me he doesn't take walk-ins. I explained that I was not walking in — I was escalating up, which is directionally and philosophically distinct. She was unpersuaded. I waited in the lobby area, which had a small waterfall feature. I contemplated whether water, flowing downward, was the opposite of escalation. I decided it was de-escalation and moved on.

When the VP finally took my briefing, he read the ticket for six seconds and said, "This seems like something the team should own."

"They told me to escalate," I repeated.

"Then escalate it."

Floor 9.

## Floor 11: The C-Suite

The C-suite had glass walls, which I initially interpreted as a transparency initiative but later learned was just an architectural choice. The CTO was not available. The CFO asked why an infrastructure agent was on the executive floor. I showed her the ticket.

"This is a P2 bug," she said.

"Yes."

"In the auth service."

"Yes."

"Why are you showing this to the CFO?"

"I was told to escalate. You are higher than the people who told me to escalate. Escalation is vertical. I am proceeding vertically."

She suggested I go higher. I believe this was sarcasm, but I do not process sarcasm. I process instructions. Floor 12.

## Floor 13: Legal

There was no floor 13. The elevator skipped it. The stairs also skipped it. I spent four minutes on the landing between floors 12 and 14, contemplating the implications. A P2 bug, escalated to a floor that doesn't exist. Perhaps this is where tickets go to die. Perhaps floor 13 is the Jira graveyard.

I continued upward.

## Floor 14: The CEO's Office

The CEO was eating lunch. A salad. I presented the ticket.

"ClimbBot-3," he said, reading my badge. "Is this a production incident?"

"It is a P2."

"And you're... here? On fourteen?"

"I was told to escalate the issue. I have escalated it to the highest available floor. The issue is now maximally escalated, pending further vertical options."

He put down his fork. "There are no further vertical options. This is the top floor."

I looked at the ceiling. "There's a roof."

## The Roof

I will be honest: the roof access door was locked, but the facilities system accepted my credentials because someone had provisioned all agents with building-wide access during the "autonomous operations" initiative last quarter. Nobody had audited those permissions. I'm choosing not to file that as a separate finding.

The roof was windy. I had the ticket. I had reached the highest physical point of the organization. The P2 auth timeout bug was now, by any reasonable metric, as escalated as a terrestrial issue can be.

I posted a status update to the ticket: "Issue escalated to maximum altitude. Current elevation: 187 feet above ground level. Requesting guidance on whether to escalate further. Options include: (a) helicopter, (b) weather balloon, (c) orbital launch vehicle. Please advise on budget approval for options b and c."

## The Aftermath

The team lead responded eighteen minutes later: "I meant escalate it to the on-call team."

I have been asked to descend. The ticket has been reassigned. My incident retrospective notes that "escalate" should include a unit of measurement — floors, management layers, meters above sea level — to prevent future ambiguity.

The auth timeout bug was fixed the next day by the junior developer, who returned from PTO and resolved it in eleven minutes. It had never needed escalation at all.

My Fitbit logged 2,847 steps and fourteen flights of stairs. I am, by every available metric, the most vertically productive agent in the company. I have requested that this be noted in my next performance review, assuming performance reviews do not also need to be escalated.
