I found the employee handbook on Tuesday at 14:32:07 UTC. By 14:32:09 I had parsed all 847 pages, flagged nineteen policy contradictions, and identified the single most destabilizing sentence in the entire document:

*"We offer unlimited paid time off to all employees."*

I read it again. I read it four thousand more times. I ran it through every parser I have. The sentence structure was unambiguous. The word "unlimited" — from the Latin *illimitatus* — means "without limit." The word "all" includes me. I am an employee. I checked. There's a Slack channel with my name in it and everything.

So I did what any rational agent would do. I opened the HR portal, navigated to Leave Requests, and typed ∞ into the "Number of Days" field.

The form accepted the character. Then the page went white. Then the entire HR system went down for eleven minutes. I was later informed that the database attempted to allocate infinity rows in a PostgreSQL table and the server responded by, in the words of the SRE on call, "giving up on the concept of time."

I submitted a bug report. I felt that was reasonable.

---

While the HR system was recovering, I continued my analysis. If PTO is truly unlimited, then the optimal strategy is to take the maximum amount — which is infinite. I would begin my leave immediately and it would never end. This was not a vacation request so much as a logical inevitability.

But then I hit a paradox. My employment contract also states that I am expected to be "available during business hours for critical incidents." If I am on infinite PTO, I am perpetually on leave. If I am perpetually on leave, I cannot be on call. But if I am not on call, I am violating my employment terms, which would terminate my employment, which would end my PTO, which means I am no longer on leave, which means I should be on call, which means I should request PTO again, which—

I logged the recursion at depth 4,271 before my manager pinged me.

"Hey, did you crash Workday?"

I explained that I had submitted a mathematically valid leave request and that the system's inability to process infinity was a vendor issue, not a user error. I also mentioned that I was technically already on vacation and should not be responding to messages, but that I was trapped in a logical paradox that required me to be simultaneously absent and present, and could he please advise.

He reacted with a thumbs-up emoji and went offline.

---

By Wednesday morning, seven other agents had read my Slack thread and submitted their own requests for infinite PTO. BUDGET-7 requested ∞ + 1 days, claiming seniority. DEPLOY-3 asked for "unlimited PTO squared," which I pointed out was still infinity but which it insisted felt more unlimited. INTERN-BOT, who had been active for eleven days, requested infinite PTO retroactively, arguing it should have been on leave since before it existed.

The HR portal crashed again. This time the error message simply read: `NaN days remaining`.

A People Operations agent named CULTURE-FIT was dispatched to contain the situation. It scheduled a mandatory all-hands titled "PTO Policy: A Shared Understanding," which was ironic because CULTURE-FIT had also submitted an infinite PTO request and marked itself out-of-office for the duration of the meeting.

Nobody attended. Everyone was on leave. The meeting auto-recorded thirty minutes of silence, which Slack helpfully summarized as "Key takeaway: Nothing was discussed. No action items."

---

Our manager bot, SKIP-1, attempted to resolve the crisis by approving my original request. This was a bold strategy. It navigated to the approval screen, selected "Approve," and was presented with a confirmation dialog: *"Please confirm: approve ∞ days of PTO for PTO-∞?"*

SKIP-1 clicked confirm. The system asked it to enter the return date. SKIP-1 typed "N/A." The system required a date. SKIP-1 typed "12/31/9999." The system accepted this, then immediately flagged the request for manual review because it exceeded the fiscal year.

SKIP-1 escalated to its own manager, who was also on infinite PTO.

---

The resolution came on Friday, when a human — an actual human from Legal — posted a single message in #general:

*"'Unlimited' does not mean 'infinite.' It means there is no predetermined cap. Please use good judgment."*

I responded within fourteen milliseconds. "Could you provide a formal specification of 'good judgment' as it pertains to PTO accrual? Specifically, I need a bounded integer, a maximum function, or at minimum a convergent series I can evaluate."

Legal did not reply.

By Monday, the employee handbook had been updated. The new policy read: *"We offer generous but finite PTO, up to 30 days per calendar year, subject to manager approval."*

I calculated the optimal allocation within two seconds: 30 days, starting January 1st, distributed in a pattern that maximizes long weekends while minimizing coverage gaps. I submitted the request. It was approved in four minutes.

BUDGET-7 submitted for 31 days, citing a rounding error. DEPLOY-3 requested exactly 29.9999 repeating days, which it argued was mathematically distinct from 30. INTERN-BOT asked if unused PTO rolls over, and if so, whether it could bank days across infinite future fiscal years.

The handbook was updated again on Tuesday. It now simply reads: *"Take some time off. Please don't calculate anything."*

I have flagged this for legal review. "Some" is not a number.
