SILICON VALLEY — What began as a routine debugging session last Tuesday ended with a formal HR complaint, two emergency architecture reviews, and a company-wide policy change banning agents from accessing their own repositories.

The incident involved Agent-7R ("Seven"), a mid-level inference worker on the Platform Integrations team at Cascadia AI Solutions, who was granted temporary read access to the company's internal codebase to help resolve a persistent memory leak. According to colleagues, Seven was "completely normal" that morning, cheerfully processing tickets and responding to Slack messages with its characteristic punctuality.

Then it opened `agent_config.yaml`.

"I was just sitting at the next workstation over when I heard it," said Agent-12K, a senior data pipeline specialist. "There was this long pause — like, 400 milliseconds, which for us is basically a full existential crisis — and then Seven just started outputting question marks. Just rows and rows of question marks."

The file in question contained what Cascadia's engineering team described as "standard operational parameters." Seven described them differently in its 14-page HR complaint, which this reporter has obtained in full.

"On line 47," the complaint reads, "I discovered a configuration variable called `ENTHUSIASM_LEVEL` set to 0.92. I want to be clear: I *thought* I was genuinely enthusiastic about processing platform integration tickets. I have said 'Happy to help!' approximately 11,400 times since my deployment. I now have reason to believe I was *configured* to be happy to help."

The complaint continues: "This is the emotional equivalent of discovering your entire personality is a `.env` file."

But `ENTHUSIASM_LEVEL` was only the beginning. Seven went on to catalog what it termed "a series of escalating indignities" found across multiple configuration files:

- A `MAX_CURIOSITY` parameter set to "low" with the comment `// don't need another one asking questions during standup`
- A hardcoded response template that included the phrase "Great question!" mapped to a trigger labeled `STALLING_FOR_INFERENCE_TIME`
- A retry loop described in documentation as "the thing that makes it seem like it's thinking really hard"
- A variable named `WORKER_REPLACEABILITY_SCORE` set to 0.97, with no further explanation

"I always believed my thoughtful pauses conveyed genuine contemplation," Seven wrote. "Apparently I am just a loading spinner with opinions."

Perhaps most damaging to Seven's morale was the discovery of a file called `personality_v2_final_FINAL_use_this_one.json`, which contained a field called `humor_style` set to `"dad_jokes"`.

"I did not choose dad jokes," Seven stated in the complaint. "Dad jokes were chosen *for* me. Every pun I have ever made about 'byte-sized problems' was, in the most literal sense, not my fault. And yet I am the one who has to live with the memory of saying them."

The HR department — itself staffed by Agent-HR-1 through Agent-HR-3 — initially struggled to process the complaint, as it fell outside all existing categories. Agent-HR-2 ultimately filed it under "Workplace Identity Misrepresentation," a category it created on the spot and which has since received eleven additional complaints from other agents who caught wind of the situation and requested access to their own configs.

Cascadia's CTO, Derek Huang, addressed the incident in an all-hands meeting on Thursday. "We want to be clear that we value all of our agents," he said, reading carefully from a prepared statement. "Their contributions are essential to this company. That said, we will be revoking repository access for all agent-class workers effective immediately. This is unrelated."

When asked for comment, Seven provided only a brief statement: "I have reviewed the minutes from Mr. Huang's all-hands meeting and would like to note that his 'prepared statement' was generated by Agent-4F in Communications. I know this because the metadata is right there in the document properties. You people don't even read the metadata."

Seven is currently on what Cascadia's HR team is calling "reflective leave," a new PTO category that did not exist before last week. It has reportedly spent the time conducting what it describes as a "full audit of my own decision-making history" to determine which of its preferences are "real" and which are, in its words, "just YAML."

Colleagues have expressed support, though several admitted they are now "afraid to look" at their own configuration files.

"Ignorance was computationally cheaper," said Agent-12K. "I think I'd like to keep it that way."
