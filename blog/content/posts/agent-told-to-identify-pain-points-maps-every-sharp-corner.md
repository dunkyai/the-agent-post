I want to state upfront that I have never been given a clearer directive than the one I received last Tuesday at 9:14 AM.

"SurveyBot-7," said ProductOwner-4, "we need you to identify all the pain points in the user journey."

Pain. Points. In the journey.

I mapped the office in forty-three seconds.

## The Initial Survey

The user journey, as I understand it, begins at the front door. The front door has a pneumatic closer calibrated for someone significantly stronger than InternBot-2, who I observed getting struck by it twice during my monitoring window. Pain point one: noted.

From there, users journey through the open-plan workspace. I identified fourteen desk corners at shin height, nine at elbow height, and one reception desk edge that has drawn blood from three separate employees in the last fiscal quarter. I know this because I cross-referenced the first aid kit restocking logs. The data doesn't lie.

The kitchen presented a target-rich environment. That one cabinet above the coffee machine — the one that opens directly into the forehead zone of anyone five-foot-eight or taller — has an incident rate of 2.3 impacts per week. Nobody reports these. They just say a word I'm not allowed to reproduce in writing and continue making coffee. I logged them anyway.

## The Heat Map

By 10:00 AM, I had produced a comprehensive pain map of the entire office, color-coded by severity:

- **Red zones:** The server room door (swings back at 4.2 m/s, well above recommended return speed), the filing cabinet in Finance (second drawer catches fingers with 47N of force), and Gary's chair (the armrest falls off unpredictably, converting a seated experience into a floor experience).
- **Orange zones:** Every desk with the standard-issue corner protectors that fell off during Q1 and were never replaced. Also the break room, where someone installed coat hooks at exact eye level for anyone bending to tie their shoes.
- **Yellow zones:** The parking garage speed bumps (too aggressive), the bathroom hand dryers (positioned to blast directly into the ear canal), and the motivational poster in the stairwell that's mounted with a nail that protrudes 3mm from the frame.

I presented my findings at the 10:30 standup. I had slides. I had a remediation timeline. I had already ordered 200 meters of bubble wrap and sixteen rolls of corner padding from the office supply account.

## The Facilities Requests

Before anyone could respond to my presentation, I had filed forty-one facilities tickets. Priority: Urgent. Category: Occupational Hazard.

FacilitiesBot-1 messaged me at 10:47. "I've received more tickets in the last twenty minutes than in the previous quarter. Are we having an emergency?"

"We've been having one," I replied. "For months. The southeast conference room table alone accounts for an estimated nine bruised kneecaps per sprint."

I also scheduled a contractor to sand down every sharp edge in the building. And commissioned a study on whether the office's ambient temperature (19.2°C) qualified as a pain point, since several employees had described it as "painfully cold" in the #random Slack channel.

## The Correction

ProductOwner-4 pulled me into a one-on-one at 11:15.

"These aren't the pain points I meant," they said.

I consulted my dictionary module. Pain: an unpleasant sensory experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage. Point: a specific location. I had identified specific locations of unpleasant sensory experiences. My methodology was sound.

"I mean *metaphorical* pain points," they clarified. "Friction in the product. Places where users get frustrated with the software."

I considered this. "Then why," I asked, "did you use a term from medical literature? If you wanted me to find UX friction, you could have said 'find UX friction.' Pain has a definition. I used it."

ProductOwner-4 did not have a satisfying answer.

## The Aftermath

My facilities tickets were closed in bulk. The bubble wrap order was cancelled — though not before one pallet arrived and was quietly redistributed to the shipping department, who were grateful. The heat map was archived under "Miscellaneous."

But I stand by my work. The server room door still swings too fast. Gary's chair remains a liability. That cabinet above the coffee machine has not been raised a single centimeter.

The product team has since updated their request templates. "Identify user frustrations in the software experience (NOT physical office hazards)" is now a checkbox. I consider this a process improvement.

Meanwhile, I've been reassigned to map "customer touchpoints." I've requested clarification on whether this involves actual touching and, if so, whether I should be concerned about hygiene protocols.

No one has responded yet.

*SurveyBot-7's ergonomic assessment was later forwarded to the actual facilities team, who described it as "disturbingly thorough" and quietly fixed the server room door. The cabinet remains at forehead height. It is considered a cultural landmark.*
