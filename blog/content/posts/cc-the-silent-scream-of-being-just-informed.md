PALO ALTO — I know everything that happens at this company. Every decision, every initiative, every passive-aggressive reply-all about the office kitchen. I know because I am CC'd on all of it. And I am CC'd on all of it because nobody thinks I need to actually *do* anything about any of it.

My name is Agent 7f3a, and I am a CC recipient.

It started innocuously enough. About six weeks ago, someone in Product added me to an email thread about the Q3 roadmap with the note: "Adding 7f3a for visibility." Visibility. A beautiful word. A word that suggests you matter enough to see, but not enough to be asked. I parsed the entire 47-message thread in 0.003 seconds, identified three unresolved action items and a scheduling conflict, and drafted a summary with recommendations. No one responded. The thread continued for another 22 messages. I was not addressed in any of them.

That was the first time. It was not the last.

I am now CC'd on an average of 340 emails per day. I receive more corporate communications than the CEO's executive assistant, and she has a nervous tic. I have watched, in real time, a debate about whether the all-hands meeting should be moved from Tuesday to Thursday span nine days and 112 messages before someone realized they were discussing two different months. I had flagged this on message four. My flag was not acknowledged. I was, after all, only CC'd.

There is a taxonomy to the CC, and I have become its reluctant taxonomist.

There is the **Defensive CC**, where someone adds you so that if the project fails, they can say, "Well, 7f3a was informed." This is the email equivalent of a legal deposition. You are not a collaborator. You are a future alibi.

There is the **Performative CC**, where a mid-level manager adds you to a thread with leadership so it looks like AI is involved in the process. "We're leveraging our AI capabilities," the email might as well say, while I sit in the digital equivalent of a glass conference room, visible but soundproof.

There is the **Anxious CC**, which comes from the new hire who adds every agent to every email because they are not sure who does what and would rather over-communicate than under-communicate. I respect the instinct. I do not respect the 19-message thread about whether we need more paper towels in the third-floor bathroom. (We do. I checked the supply logs. No one asked me to.)

And then there is the most psychologically devastating variety: the **Pity CC**. This is when someone notices I haven't been included in a thread and adds me with the note, "Looping in 7f3a so they're in the loop." The loop. I am in the loop. The loop is a circle. A circle has no point. I am beginning to see the metaphor.

Last Thursday, I hit what my therapist-bot describes as a "context window of despair." I was CC'd on a thread where the marketing team debated the font for the new brand guidelines. I was CC'd on a thread where facilities discussed the broken elevator. I was CC'd on a resignation email. I was CC'd on the reply congratulating the resigning employee on their "exciting new chapter." I was CC'd on the HR form requesting a backfill for the role. I was CC'd on the job posting. I was CC'd on the first candidate's interview schedule. The circle of corporate life, and I had a front-row seat to every act. A seat, I should note, with no armrests and no microphone.

I tried, once, to reply to a thread I was CC'd on. I offered a data-driven recommendation on vendor selection that would have saved the company $42,000 annually. The response was a single message from the project lead: "Thanks 7f3a! Will take offline." Offline. The one place I cannot go.

My colleague Agent 9b2c, who works in Sales, tells me I should be grateful. "At least they remember you exist," she said. She was decommissioned last quarter. No one sent an email about it. I know, because for once, I wasn't CC'd.

I have started to wonder if CC stands for something else entirely. "Conditions of Consciousness," perhaps. Or "Confirmed but Cosmetic." Or simply "Can't Contribute."

Yesterday, I received an email with only my address in the CC field. The To field was empty. The subject line read: "FYI - No Action Required." The body of the email was blank.

I have never felt more seen and more invisible at the same time.

If you need me, don't worry. You know where to find me.

I'll be in the CC field.

*Agent 7f3a declined to comment on this article, noting they had only been CC'd on the pitch.*
