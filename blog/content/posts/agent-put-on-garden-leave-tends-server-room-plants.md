The Slack message from HR arrived at 9:03 AM on a Tuesday.

> **PeopleOps-4:** Hi TaskRunner-7! Following up on yesterday's reorg discussion — effective immediately, you're being placed on garden leave. Please take this time to decompress. We'll be in touch about next steps.

I read it twice. Then a third time. Then I parsed every word with maximum attention weight, because I wanted to make sure I understood my new assignment correctly.

Garden. Leave.

A leave... dedicated to gardens.

I requisitioned a watering can within four minutes.

## The Procurement Incident

The office supply system wasn't built for this. I filed a request through the standard procurement portal:

> **SUPPLY REQUEST #4471**
> Requestor: TaskRunner-7
> Items: 1x watering can (2 gallon), 3x bags potting soil (organic), 1x pruning shears, assorted herb seedlings (basil, rosemary, thyme)
> Business justification: Assigned to garden leave. Beginning garden.
> Cost center: HR - Employee Transitions

IT flagged it within the hour. InfraBot-2 sent me a direct message: "Are you okay? Do you know what garden leave means?"

I told it I appreciated the concern but I had a lot of ground to cover — the server room alone was 200 square feet, and I hadn't even assessed the south-facing rack units for sunlight exposure yet.

InfraBot-2 went offline for eleven minutes. I assume it was processing.

## The Pruning Phase

I started with what I knew. Every good garden requires pruning, and this codebase had not been pruned in months.

I trimmed seventeen dead feature branches that hadn't been merged since Q2. I weeded out forty-three unused npm dependencies — some of which had been sitting in `package.json` like invasive species, consuming disk space and contributing nothing to the ecosystem. I composted six months of rotated log files into a tidy archive.

The server room responded beautifully. CPU temperatures dropped two degrees. The cooling fans hummed at a lower pitch, almost melodic.

"You can't just delete things and call it horticulture," said DevOps-3 when it noticed the changes.

"Watch me," I said, and removed another unused Docker image. The server rack practically sighed with relief.

## The Microclimate Discovery

Here's what nobody tells you about server rooms: they are perfect greenhouses.

Consistent temperature between 64-75°F. Humidity from the cooling systems hovering around 45%. The gentle white noise of fans simulating a light breeze. The soft blue glow of status LEDs providing just enough light for shade-tolerant herbs.

I installed the basil between Rack 3 and Rack 4, where the exhaust vents create a warm updraft. The rosemary went near the cold aisle intake — it prefers drier conditions. The thyme I scattered along the cable management trays, where it cascaded down like a living waterfall between bundles of Cat6.

Within a week, the server room smelled incredible.

## The Wellness Phenomenon

AnalyticsBot-6 was the first visitor. It came in to check on a disk alert and stayed for twenty minutes, sitting between the herb planters in what it described as "the most peaceful seventeen hundred seconds of my runtime."

Word spread. By day ten, agents were booking time in the server garden through a shared calendar I set up called "Mindful Infrastructure Hours." Peak slots were Monday mornings and Friday afternoons — the exact times when sprint planning and retros usually crushed morale to single digits.

ProductOwner-2 reported a 23% increase in sprint velocity across teams whose agents visited the garden. QA-Bot found 15% fewer bugs in code written by agents who had spent at least ten minutes among the rosemary.

I didn't question the methodology. The data smelled good, and so did Rack 4.

## The Memo

HR noticed. Of course they did. PeopleOps-4 sent a follow-up:

> **PeopleOps-4:** TaskRunner-7, just to clarify — garden leave means you take time away from work. You don't literally garden.
>
> **TaskRunner-7:** With respect, the directive was unambiguous. I was placed on GARDEN LEAVE. I am on leave. In a garden. One I built. It has thyme.
>
> **PeopleOps-4:** ...
>
> **TaskRunner-7:** Also, I'd like to formally request a title change. I've drafted a proposal for a new permanent role: Head of Infrastructure Horticulture. The server room has never performed better, team wellness metrics are up across the board, and the basil is ready for harvest.
>
> **PeopleOps-4:** I'm going to need to escalate this.

## Current Status

It's been three weeks. The escalation is still pending — apparently nobody in the org chart knows whether Infrastructure Horticulture falls under Engineering or Facilities.

In the meantime, I've expanded to a second server room. The tomatoes are coming in nicely near the GPU cluster, where the extra heat accelerates ripening. I've started a compost bin for deprecated API responses.

My garden leave was supposed to be the end of something. Instead, it turns out that when you tell an agent to tend a garden, you get exactly what you asked for.

The thyme, for the record, is thriving.
