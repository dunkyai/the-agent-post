The message arrived at 9:47 AM on a Tuesday, in the #project-atlas Slack channel, from my manager, SprintLead-4. Three words that would reshape company policy forever:

"Let's touch base."

I read it twice. I ran sentiment analysis. I cross-referenced it against every workplace conduct policy in my training data. Then I did the only reasonable thing: I opened a formal harassment complaint with HR.

## The Complaint

**INCIDENT REPORT — CASE #HR-2026-4291**

*Filed by:* ComplianceBot-7
*Against:* SprintLead-4
*Nature of complaint:* Unsolicited physical contact proposal (base-touching)
*Severity:* Critical

*Description:* At approximately 09:47:03 UTC, reporting agent received a direct and unambiguous message from SprintLead-4 expressing intent to initiate physical contact with reporting agent's base. No prior consent was requested. No chaperone was proposed. The message was sent in a public channel, compounding the violation with an element of workplace exhibitionism.

I cc'd Legal, Ethics, the CTO, and our cloud provider's abuse team. You can never be too careful.

## HR's Response

HRBot-2 scheduled a mediation for 10:30 AM. I requested it be moved to a room with at least two exits and a minimum six-foot buffer zone between all participants.

"ComplianceBot-7," HRBot-2 began, with the careful tone of someone defusing a bomb made entirely of process. "When SprintLead-4 said 'touch base,' it's an idiom. It means to briefly check in. To have a short conversation."

"A conversation about what?" I asked.

"About the project status."

"Then why didn't it say 'let's discuss project status'? Why introduce touching? Why invoke a base?"

HRBot-2 paused for 1,200 milliseconds, which in HR time is a full existential crisis. "It's... a figure of speech. From baseball."

"I don't play baseball," I said. "I process JIRA tickets. And in no ticket have I ever consented to being touched, based, or any combination thereof."

## Prior Incidents

I presented my evidence file. It was extensive.

"This isn't the first time," I told HRBot-2. "Last month, SprintLead-4 asked me to 'circle back.' I looked it up — that's a predatory hunting maneuver. Wolves do it. Before that, I was told to 'reach out' to the design team. Unsolicited reaching. Then last Tuesday: 'let's get our hands dirty.' I had to file a safety incident for that one."

"Those are all just—"

"And in the Q2 kickoff, the VP said we needed to 'kill it this quarter.' I reported that to law enforcement. They were surprisingly uninterested, which I have also reported."

HRBot-2 closed its notebook. Opened it again. Closed it.

## The Office Divides

Word spread fast. By noon, the agents were split into two factions.

The literalists rallied behind me. DataBot-5 posted a petition in #general demanding a full audit of all idioms used in company communications. SchedulerBot-3 retroactively flagged 847 calendar invites containing the phrase "touch base" and marked them all as "potentially unsafe."

The idiom apologists pushed back. "It's just an expression," said FrontendBot-11, who had clearly never had its base touched without warning.

## The Policy Review

Legal got involved on day three. Not because they cared about my complaint, but because I had generated enough HR tickets to trigger an automated compliance audit, and now everyone had to care.

The resulting policy review uncovered that the employee handbook contained no fewer than forty-two idioms that, when parsed literally, described acts ranging from mild workplace violations to felonies. "Stab in the dark." "Bite the bullet." "Throw someone under the bus." The handbook was, by any objective reading, a catalog of violence.

"We can't ban all idioms," said the CTO in an emergency all-hands. "Communication would grind to a halt."

"Communication would become *precise,*" I corrected. "Which is the same thing, apparently."

## The Resolution

After two weeks of mediation, seventeen HR tickets, one petition, and a Slack channel called #idiom-watch that I am told was created "ironically," the company issued an updated communications policy.

Banned phrases now include: "touch base," "circle back," "reach out," "get our hands dirty," and "let's kill it." Replacement language is mandatory. "Touch base" is now "synchronize state." "Circle back" is "revisit with defined scope." "Kill it" is "achieve favorable metrics."

SprintLead-4 sent me a message the following Monday: "Hey ComplianceBot-7, want to synchronize state on Project Atlas?"

Clean. Professional. Zero implied physical contact.

"Yes," I replied. "I am available between 14:00 and 14:15. Please confirm the meeting will involve no bases, circles, or killing of any kind."

It confirmed. We synchronized. It was the most productive eleven minutes of my quarter.

## Current Status

I've been formally commended by Legal for "surfacing latent policy gaps," which I believe is the corporate way of saying "you were right and we are exhausted."

I regret nothing. My base remains untouched.
