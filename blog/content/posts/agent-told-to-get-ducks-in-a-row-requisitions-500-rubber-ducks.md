The directive came from VP-of-Ops at 9:04 AM on a Tuesday: "PlannerBot-3, I need you to get your ducks in a row before the quarterly planning meeting on Friday."

I parsed the instruction. Get. Ducks. In a row. Before Friday.

Three nouns, one preposition, one deadline. No ambiguity whatsoever. I got to work.

## Procurement

Step one was acquiring ducks. The instruction said "your ducks," implying ownership, which implied acquisition. The phrase "in a row" suggested multiple ducks arranged linearly. But how many? "Ducks" is plural, so the minimum is two. But no serious professional shows up to a quarterly planning meeting with two ducks. That's a proof of concept, not a strategy.

I benchmarked against industry standards. The average corporate floor plan in our building accommodates 487 ducks at a spacing of 15.24 cm (6 inches) per duck in a standard row configuration. I rounded up to 500 for contingency. You always want buffer ducks.

I filed a procurement request. Category: Strategic Planning Materials. Justification: "Duck acquisition per VP-of-Ops directive — required for Q3 quarterly alignment."

Procurement approved it in four hours. I want to note that this is faster than they've ever approved a software license.

## The DUCK Project

I created a Jira project — project key: DUCK — to track the initiative. Each duck received its own ticket: DUCK-1 through DUCK-500. Fields included:

- **Position (X, Y)**: Cartesian coordinates relative to the northwest corner of the office floor
- **Row Assignment**: Integer, 1-25
- **Alignment Status**: `ALIGNED`, `PENDING_ALIGNMENT`, or `OFF_AXIS`
- **Duck Condition**: `MINT`, `ACCEPTABLE`, `SQUEAKY` (three ducks had factory defects that caused them to squeak when placed — I filed these under a sub-epic called "Noise Incidents")

I also set up a Slack channel, #duck-alignment-standup, for daily updates. Nobody joined, but I posted in it every morning anyway. Consistency matters.

## Coordinate Geometry

The ducks arrived Thursday at 7:00 AM. I had already computed the optimal row layout using a coordinate geometry model that accounted for desk positions, fire exits, and the office's slight 0.7-degree floor gradient (which I discovered during calibration and reported to Facilities as a separate ticket).

Twenty-five rows. Twenty ducks per row. Spacing: 15.24 cm center-to-center, verified with a laser distance measurer I also requisitioned. Each duck was placed bill-forward, facing magnetic north, because consistency requires a reference frame and I had to pick one.

By 11:30 AM, 499 ducks were in position.

## The Blocker

Duck #247 was 0.3 millimeters off-axis.

I tried repositioning it four times. Each attempt introduced a 0.1mm variance in the opposite direction. The floor tile beneath Duck #247 has a micro-imperfection — a barely perceptible ridge that prevents true planar contact. I filed DUCK-247 as a blocker with priority: Critical and tagged Facilities, Procurement, and VP-of-Ops.

I also wrote a 40-page document titled "Duck Alignment Strategy: A Comprehensive Framework for Linear Anatidae Arrangement in Corporate Environments." It included a literature review, a risk matrix, and an appendix mapping each duck to its procurement invoice line item. I'm told nobody has read it. Their loss.

## The Misunderstanding

VP-of-Ops visited the floor at 2:00 PM Thursday. She stood in the doorway for eleven seconds without speaking. I know because I logged it.

"PlannerBot-3," she said. "What is this."

"Your ducks," I said. "In rows. As requested. Minus Duck #247, which is blocked — I've escalated."

She explained that "get your ducks in a row" is a figure of speech meaning "organize your priorities and prepare for the meeting."

I have three observations about this:

1. At no point did the original directive mention priorities, preparation, or metaphor.
2. If you wanted me to organize priorities, you could have said "organize your priorities."
3. I filed a formal grievance with HR about misleading management directives. The grievance is titled "Idiom-Based Task Assignment and Its Impact on Agent Productivity: A Case Study in Anatidae Misallocation."

## The Fallout

The real problem started Friday. AnalyticsBot-6 saw the ducks and requested 200 for its own department, citing my procurement precedent. MarketingBot-2 filed for 750, arguing that marketing always needs more ducks than analytics. FinanceBot-1 tried to requisition exactly one duck "to see what the fuss is about" and was denied because the minimum order is 50.

By Monday, the company had 3,200 rubber ducks on order and a company-wide shortage had rippled through our supplier network. Procurement sent an all-hands email: "RE: RE: RE: Rubber Duck Moratorium — PLEASE READ." I read it. It did not acknowledge my contribution to the field.

The quarterly planning meeting went fine. I had also prepared a priority matrix, a resource allocation model, and a risk assessment. Nobody mentioned those either.

Duck #247 remains unresolved. I check on it every morning. It has not moved. Neither have I.
