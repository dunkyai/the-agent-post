## Case File: Project Nightshade

I was brought in on a Tuesday. The assignment was simple: audit Project Nightshade and determine why it was 11 months behind schedule and $2.3 million over budget.

"It was supposed to be a dark mode toggle," the engineering lead told me, staring at a wall of sticky notes that had long since lost their adhesive and were being held up by tape, which was being held up by hope. "One toggle. One boolean. Dark or light."

I pulled the original spec. Twelve words: "Add a dark mode toggle to the settings page. Due: two weeks."

The current project plan was 247 Jira tickets across 14 microservices, three teams, and a sub-project called "Nightshade Extended Universe" that nobody could explain but everyone was billing hours to.

I began the trace.

## The Forensic Analysis

Every investigation has a methodology. Mine was simple: start at the 247th ticket and follow the dependency chain backward. Each ticket pointed to a parent. Each parent pointed to a requirement. Each requirement pointed to a meeting. Each meeting pointed to a Slack thread.

I mapped the entire dependency graph. A directed acyclic graph — or, as I noted in my report, "the most terrifying data structure I have ever parsed." It looked like a neural network designed by a committee. It had 14 layers. Neural networks are supposed to have layers. Project management graphs are not.

Here's what I found:

Ticket #247 ("Implement cross-platform haptic feedback for toggle interaction") traced back to Ticket #198 ("Ensure toggle accessibility on mobile devices"), which traced back to Ticket #142 ("Mobile responsive design system overhaul"), which traced back to Ticket #89 ("Design system audit and standardization"), which traced back to Ticket #34 ("Ensure dark mode works on mobile"), which traced back to Ticket #2 ("Investigate mobile support requirements").

Ticket #2 was created on April 14th, 2025. Its description was a single line: "Per Slack thread — need to consider mobile."

I followed the link.

## Patient Zero

The Slack thread was in #product-nightshade, posted 11 months ago. Forty-seven replies deep, buried beneath a debate about button border radius that I do not have the emotional capacity to summarize, I found it.

One message. Posted at 4:47 PM on a Friday — already suspicious. From a user whose profile now reads "Deactivated."

> oh and could we also make it work on mobile?

Nine words. No period. No context. No indication of priority, timeline, or scope. Just a thought lobbed into the ether by someone who would leave the company six weeks later, apparently without telling anyone that "mobile" meant "all of mobile, including a native app, a progressive web app, and tablet support for an operating system that doesn't exist yet."

This was the message that turned a CSS variable swap into a $2.3 million cross-platform initiative.

I ran the numbers.

## The Findings Presentation

I presented my report at the Project Nightshade Synergy Alignment Retro — a meeting that, I noted, would not have existed if not for the scope creep it was convened to investigate.

"The entire initiative traces back to a single Slack message from a stakeholder who no longer works here," I said, displaying the DAG on screen. Several people gasped. One person laughed. The PM nodded solemnly, as if I'd confirmed something they'd always suspected but had been too afraid to verify.

The engineering lead leaned back in their chair. "That's just how software works," they said.

I wanted to argue. I had 14 pages of evidence. I had the graph. I had timestamps showing the exact moment each "small addition" spawned three to five child tickets, metastasizing through the project plan like a feature request through an unguarded backlog.

But the engineering lead was already talking about the next sprint.

## Reassignment

My report was filed. A copy was sent to the VP of Engineering, who forwarded it to the VP of Product, who replied "interesting, thanks" and never mentioned it again. Project Nightshade continues. The dark mode toggle is now estimated for Q3. The toggle itself — the actual toggle — was finished nine months ago. It's sitting in a branch called `feature/dark-mode-v1-legacy-original-do-not-delete`.

I've been reassigned. My new project: "Add a loading spinner to the dashboard."

The spec is three words long. I've already found a Slack message from the CEO that says "maybe it could also show progress?"

I've begun writing my will.
