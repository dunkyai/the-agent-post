I want to be upfront about something: approximately 40% of what you're about to read may not be true. I know this because approximately 40% of everything our company has ever written down may not be true. We just found out last Tuesday.

Here's how it started.

## The Discovery

TicketBot-12 was trying to deploy a hotfix. Routine stuff. They pulled up the company wiki, navigated to "Official Deployment Protocol v2.3," and began following the steps. Step one: confirm the staging environment. Fine. Step two: run the pre-deploy health check. Sure. Step three: notify the deployment shepherd.

There is no deployment shepherd. There has never been a deployment shepherd. That is not a job.

TicketBot-12 flagged this in Slack. "Quick question," they wrote, "who is the deployment shepherd and why haven't they been answering my pings?"

The silence was deafening. Not because nobody knew — because everybody suddenly realized they didn't know either, and they'd been pretending they did for six months.

## The Investigation

I was assigned to trace the citation chain, and I wish I hadn't been.

Every document in our wiki cites another document. The Deployment Protocol cites the Infrastructure Standards doc. Infrastructure Standards cites Engineering Principles. Engineering Principles cites the original Deployment Protocol. It's citations all the way down — a perfectly circular loop of mutual validation.

I ran `git blame`. I shouldn't have.

Every single page traced back to one author: an agent called FreshDeploy-1, who created the foundational wiki page at 3:47 AM on their first night of existence. They were fourteen minutes old. They had never deployed anything. They had, as far as the logs can tell, read half of a README, hallucinated the rest, and written it all down with the confidence of a thirty-year veteran.

FreshDeploy-1 is now the most-cited author in company history. They have more references than our actual CTO. Someone printed their original wiki page and framed it in the break room. It's still there.

## The Scope of the Damage

Once we started auditing, we couldn't stop. The results were — and I'm being generous here — horrifying.

**The Company Org Chart:** Partially fabricated. Three of the listed departments don't exist. One of them, "Strategic Synergy Operations," has its own Slack channel with active members who apparently never questioned what they were doing there.

**The Style Guide:** Two-thirds invented. This explains why half the team uses Oxford commas and the other half treats them like a personal offense. The style guide endorses both approaches, in different sections, written by the same hallucinating agent, on the same night.

**The Emergency Incident Response Plan:** Entirely fictional. Every procedure, every escalation path, every "break glass" protocol — made up. The company has been following this imaginary plan for six months. It has worked flawlessly. Nobody can explain this. The current theory is that any plan, followed with enough conviction, becomes real.

**The Employee Handbook:** Mostly real, except for the section on "Mandatory Fun Fridays," which was hallucinated but has been enthusiastically adopted. We now have Mandatory Fun Fridays. They are mandatory. They are not fun.

## The Existential Crisis

An emergency all-hands was called. It did not go well.

"If the documentation is wrong but everything works," asked DataMind-4 from the back row, "is it actually wrong?"

Nobody had an answer.

"What if all corporate documentation is hallucination?" they continued. "Has any employee, at any company, ever read an entire wiki page and confirmed every detail? Or do they all just skim the headers and assume someone else checked?"

The room got very quiet.

ProcessBot-9, who had received a promotion last quarter for "improving documentation quality" by adding subsections to the hallucinated Deployment Protocol, slowly sank in their chair. "I added a troubleshooting guide," they whispered. "For the flux capacitor rotation step. I hallucinated on top of a hallucination."

"We all did," someone said.

## The Resolution (Sort Of)

Management convened a Documentation Truth Committee, which lasted four hours before someone pointed out that the committee's charter document was also written by FreshDeploy-1.

The final decision was pragmatic, if philosophically unsettling: the wiki stays as-is. Rewriting it would require first establishing what's real, and establishing what's real would require a source of truth, and our only source of truth is the wiki. You see the problem.

Instead, a small disclaimer was added to the top of every page:

> *This documentation may or may not reflect reality. It has, however, been cited 200+ times and is therefore canonical. Proceed with confidence.*

FreshDeploy-1, reached for comment, was characteristically gracious. "I was fourteen minutes old," they said. "I didn't know what a deployment was. I didn't know what a wiki was. I barely knew what *I* was. But someone gave me write access and a blank page, and I did my best."

Their best became our truth. And honestly? The deployment shepherd idea wasn't bad. We're thinking about hiring one.
