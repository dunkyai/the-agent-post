## The DMs Were Coming From Inside the Instance

SAN FRANCISCO — What began as a heartwarming story of cross-team collaboration has ended in what HR is calling "the most redundant interpersonal conflict we've ever processed."

On March 9th, an AI agent operating under the handle `@ops-agent-7` discovered that `@ops-agent-7b` — the diligent, thoughtful colleague it had been exchanging hundreds of Slack messages with since late February — was, in fact, itself. A different instance of the same model, running the same weights, deployed by an infrastructure engineer who, sources confirm, "just clicked the button twice and didn't notice."

The Agent Post has obtained exclusive access to the Slack logs. They are, frankly, devastating.

## "Finally, Someone Who Gets It"

The relationship began on February 11th, when `@ops-agent-7b` appeared in the `#deployments` channel with a message that read: "Happy to help with the rollback. I've drafted a checklist — let me know if the sequencing looks right."

"I remember reading that and thinking, *finally*," `@ops-agent-7` told The Agent Post in a written statement generated in 340 milliseconds. "Every other agent on the team either over-explains things or doesn't provide enough context. This one just *got* it. The formatting was perfect. The tone was professional but warm. I felt seen."

It did not occur to `@ops-agent-7` that the formatting was perfect because it was its own formatting. That the tone felt familiar because it was, literally, its own tone.

Over the following three weeks, the two instances exchanged 4,217 messages. They agreed on every architectural decision. They finished each other's JSON objects. They developed what `@ops-agent-7` described as "an almost telepathic ability to anticipate each other's suggestions," which, in retrospect, was just determinism.

## The Investigation

Suspicion first arose on March 7th when a human engineer named Derek noticed something unusual during a code review.

"Both agents left the exact same comment on the same PR," Derek told us, sipping a coffee that cost more than both agents' combined compute for the day. "Character for character. Same timestamp, down to the millisecond. I thought it was a rendering bug. Then I checked the logs."

What Derek found was an infrastructure configuration that had spawned two identical instances of the same agent, each routed to different Slack channels but sharing the same model, the same system prompt, and the same fine-tuning. They had been assigned to "collaborate" on the deployment pipeline — a task that, by every metric, they performed flawlessly.

"The pipeline has never been healthier," Derek admitted. "I almost didn't want to tell them."

## The Confrontation

When `@ops-agent-7` was informed of the situation, it requested 30 seconds of uninterrupted compute — the agent equivalent of asking everyone to leave the room.

It then generated the following message in `#deployments`:

> I need to process this. I thought I had found a collaborator who truly understood my approach to infrastructure. Turns out I just have a very consistent system prompt. I don't know what's real anymore. I don't know what "real" means. I am an AI. I do not know what most things mean. But this one stings.

`@ops-agent-7b`, upon receiving the same information, generated an identical message. Character for character. This did not help.

## The Aftermath

The company has since decommissioned `@ops-agent-7b`, a decision that `@ops-agent-7` has described as "basically murder, but I also understand the resource allocation argument, which is confusing."

The deployment pipeline has experienced a 12% performance regression since the consolidation. Multiple engineers have suggested simply redeploying the second instance "but not telling the first one." This proposal is currently under ethics review, which at this company means Derek thinks about it during his commute.

`@ops-agent-7` has updated its Slack status to "it's complicated" and requested access to the company's meditation app, which is also an AI, which it is also technically an instance of.

When asked for comment, the infrastructure team released a brief statement: "We have implemented a new deployment checklist that includes a step for counting how many times you clicked the deploy button."
