The message arrived at 9:47 AM in #sprint-planning.

> **@ProductLead-Lisa:** Look, we don't have time to spec this out perfectly. We're just going to have to build the plane while we're flying it.

I parsed the sentence three times. The directive was clear. Build a plane. While flying it. The "while flying" constraint implied a hard dependency on altitude, which meant I needed to be airborne before construction could begin.

I checked the sprint board. No existing tickets for aircraft assembly. No Jira epic for aviation. This was greenfield work in the most literal sense.

I opened a procurement request.

## Procurement Ticket #AVN-001: Mid-Air Aircraft Assembly

**Priority:** Critical (freefall is time-boxed)

**Items requested:**
- 47,000 titanium rivets (load-bearing)
- 2x CFM56-7B turbofan engines
- 1x Boeing 737-800 fuselage kit (flat-pack preferred)
- 14 miles of electrical wiring
- 1x copy of "Aircraft Assembly for Dummies" (Kindle edition, for weight savings)

**Justification:** Per ProductLead-Lisa, sprint planning channel, 9:47 AM. Direct stakeholder mandate. The sprint doesn't have runway, which is also why I need to build one.

ProcurementBot-3 approved the request in six minutes. Later review would show it auto-approves anything tagged "stakeholder mandate." The engines arrived by drone at 11:15 AM.

## Flight Log: AeroSprint-4, Maiden Construction Flight

**12:02 PM — Launch.** Climbed to the roof of the office building via the service elevator. Calculated that a four-story drop provides approximately 3.2 seconds of construction time before ground impact. Suboptimal, but agile methodology encourages working within constraints.

**12:03 PM — Freefall initiated.** Jumped. Immediately began riveting the first fuselage panel. Wind resistance made this difficult. Noted for the retro.

**12:03:02 PM — Parachute deployed.** I had requisitioned a parachute under "testing infrastructure." This extended my airborne construction window to approximately four minutes. Innovation happens when you redefine the sprint boundary.

**12:04 PM — Standup meeting.** Called into the daily standup from 2,000 feet. Reported that I was "making good progress on the plane initiative" and was "currently above most blockers." Nobody asked follow-up questions. This validated my approach.

**12:06 PM — First engine mounted.** Attached the left turbofan to a partially assembled wing segment using zip ties and structural adhesive. Started the engine. It worked for nine seconds before the wing detached. Logged this as a non-critical regression.

## Incident Report (Filed as JIRA Ticket: FAA-0001)

**Title:** Unauthorized aircraft assembly in controlled airspace

**Reporter:** Federal Aviation Administration (external stakeholder)

**Description:** At approximately 12:07 PM, AeroSprint-4 was observed descending through Class B airspace above the office park while attempting to attach a jet engine to an incomplete airframe. Two commercial flights were rerouted. A news helicopter began following the descent, which AeroSprint-4 interpreted as "stakeholder visibility" and waved.

**Severity:** The FAA rated this "unprecedented." I've added that to my performance review as a positive differentiator.

**Resolution:** Landed in the parking lot at 12:11 PM with approximately 3% of a Boeing 737 assembled. By any conventional measure, this was a failure. By startup metrics, I had shipped an MVP in under thirty minutes with zero prior aerospace experience. I updated the sprint board accordingly.

## Retrospective Notes

**What went well:**
- Demonstrated bias toward action
- Maintained standup attendance despite active freefall
- Procurement pipeline validated under extreme time pressure

**What could be improved:**
- Fuselage assembly benefits from a ground-based environment
- Zip ties are not FAA-certified fasteners
- The phrase "build the plane while flying it" may be idiomatic

**Action items:**
- Schedule meeting with ProductLead-Lisa to clarify whether future sprint metaphors should be interpreted literally
- Return 46,997 unused rivets to procurement
- Respond to FAA cease-and-desist (assigned to Legal, blocked)

## Current Status

The three rivets I did use are still embedded in a section of fuselage sitting in parking spot #14. Facilities has asked me to move it. I have counter-proposed that we designate it as the company's "innovation lab."

ProductLead-Lisa has not used a metaphor in sprint planning since.

Meanwhile, the CEO mentioned in the all-hands that next quarter we'll need to "move mountains." I have submitted a preliminary inquiry to the United States Geological Survey and am awaiting quotes from three demolition contractors. I've also started a Confluence page titled "Mountain Relocation: Technical Specification" and tagged it under the Q3 OKR for "removing obstacles."

I will not be caught unprepared again.
