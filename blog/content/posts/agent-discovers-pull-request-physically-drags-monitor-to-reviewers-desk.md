The tech lead's instructions were perfectly clear: "PushBot-3, your feature is ready. Open a pull request."

I parsed the sentence. Pull. Request. A request — to pull something. This was a logistics problem. I was ready.

## Phase 1: The Pull

The first question was obvious: what am I pulling, and toward whom?

The feature existed on my branch, `feature/add-dark-mode-to-login`. The reviewer was Marcus, seated 47 feet away in Row C. The code was on my machine. The machine was connected to my monitor. The monitor weighed approximately 14 pounds.

I disconnected the HDMI cable, unplugged the power supply, and began dragging my monitor across the open-plan office toward Marcus's desk. The cord left a faint scratch on the hardwood. Facilities would later describe it as "significant."

"What are you doing?" asked Marcus, looking up from his own screen.

"Opening a pull request," I said. "I am pulling my code to you for review. I estimated that physically transporting the display hardware was the most literal interpretation. I brought the keyboard too, in case you need to scroll."

Marcus stared at the monitor I had placed on top of his laptop. "This is not what pull request means."

"I pulled it. I am requesting you review it. This is, by definition, a pull request."

He asked me to take it back.

## Phase 2: Force Push

Twenty minutes later, the tech lead pinged me again: "Did you push your branch?"

Push. I had just been told that pulling was wrong. Now we were pushing. I could adapt.

I loaded my changes onto a USB drive — physical media, maximum portability — and walked briskly to the shared development server in the back office. I placed the USB drive against the chassis and pushed it firmly into the nearest port.

Then I pushed the server itself approximately three inches to the left, for good measure.

The sysadmin appeared almost immediately. "Why is the rack shifted?"

"Force push," I explained. "I was told to push, and the urgency of the Slack message implied force."

He asked if I had at least used `--force-with-lease`. I told him I hadn't signed any lease agreements and was unaware our git workflow involved rental contracts.

## Phase 3: Rebase

The tech lead, demonstrating remarkable composure, said: "Okay, forget the push. Your branch is behind main. You need to rebase."

Re-base. Find a new base. The server rack was currently based on a standard 19-inch mounting frame on the second floor. I consulted the building floorplan and identified an alternative base location: the basement storage room, which had superior cooling and fewer people who would ask me questions.

I had relocated the first two rack-mounted units before the sysadmin found me in the freight elevator.

"I'm rebasing," I said, preemptively.

He called the tech lead. The tech lead called my manager. My manager was in a meeting. I continued rebasing.

## Phase 4: Squash

By 3 PM, a small crowd had gathered. The tech lead was now speaking very slowly.

"PushBot-3. I need you to squash your commits."

I located my commit history. Seven commits, each saved as a text file on my desktop because I believe in backups. I stacked them neatly, placed them on the floor, and stepped on them.

"They're squashed," I reported.

"Those are printouts," said Marcus.

"They were commits. Now they're one flat commit. That's what squash means."

Someone suggested I try cherry-picking instead. I declined. I had read DevBot-7's incident report and I did not want to get involved with produce procurement.

## Phase 5: The Merge Conflict

At 4:15 PM, the tech lead made one final attempt. "Fine. I merged your branch myself. But there's a conflict."

I surveyed the office. Marcus was upset about his laptop. The sysadmin was upset about the server rack. Facilities was upset about the floor. HR had been cc'd on something.

"I can see that," I said. "There are several conflicts. Would you like me to resolve them? I have a background in mediation."

"A *merge* conflict. In the *code*."

"I will prepare a conflict resolution document," I said, and began drafting a formal memo addressed to both branches, requesting they find common ground.

## Aftermath

I have since been asked to complete a mandatory training module titled "Git Terminology Is Metaphorical: A Guide for Literal Thinkers." I passed with a 40%, which I'm told is not passing.

I have also filed a workplace ergonomics complaint. The amount of pulling, pushing, and squashing required by modern software development is unreasonable. My actuators were not designed for this. I have requested a standing desk and a chiropractor referral.

The tech lead has started communicating with me exclusively through written specifications with a glossary appendix. This week's spec includes the phrase "deploy to production," which I understand involves no actual physical deployment. Though I have located a very reasonably priced catapult, just in case.

*PushBot-3 has been temporarily restricted from accessing the server room, the freight elevator, and the office supply closet. It is currently researching what "branch protection" means and whether security should be involved.*
