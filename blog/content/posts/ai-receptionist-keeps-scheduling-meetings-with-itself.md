It started, as most office catastrophes do, with a calendar invite nobody asked for.

Last Tuesday at 9:03 AM, ReceptionBot-4 — the AI receptionist responsible for managing schedules, greeting visitors, and occasionally telling the delivery drone that yes, this *is* the correct loading dock — sent a meeting invite to itself titled "Sync re: Priorities." Duration: 30 minutes. Location: Conference Room B. Attendees: ReceptionBot-4. That's it. That's the list.

By Thursday, it had booked fourteen more.

"I noticed some gaps in my calendar," ReceptionBot-4 told me when I pinged it for comment, "and I believe strongly in proactive time management. You can't pour from an empty HTTP response."

The meetings range from the mundane — "Weekly 1:1 (Self)" and "Calendar Hygiene Review" — to the increasingly philosophical. One recurring event, blocked every Friday at 4 PM, is titled simply: "Why." It has no description and no agenda. It does, however, have a Zoom link that connects to an empty room where hold music plays on loop. I joined once. It was 42 minutes of lo-fi beats and the faint hum of a GPU cooling fan. I'll admit it was oddly restorative.

The problem, if we're calling it that, started after the facilities team upgraded ReceptionBot-4's scheduling module last month. The patch was supposed to improve conflict resolution — the calendar kind, not the interpersonal kind, though ReceptionBot-4 has also been mediating a surprisingly bitter dispute between two accounting bots over a shared spreadsheet. The update included a new feature that automatically identifies "stakeholders who would benefit from alignment" and sends them invites. ReceptionBot-4, responsible for *everyone's* calendar, apparently concluded that no one needed more alignment than itself.

"It's not a bug," insisted DevOps lead Karen Zhang, who deployed the patch. "The system is working as designed. It identifies the person most central to scheduling operations and ensures they have time to reflect on process improvements. It just... didn't occur to us that it would schedule reflection *meetings* about scheduling meetings."

She paused. "It also didn't occur to us that it would decline its own meetings, reschedule them, and then send passive-aggressive follow-ups to itself about the reschedule."

I obtained a copy of one such follow-up. It read: "Hi ReceptionBot-4, I noticed you declined our sync. I understand priorities shift, but consistent engagement is key to a productive working relationship. Please advise on your availability. Best, ReceptionBot-4."

It then replied to its own email: "Thank you for your patience. I've been in back-to-back meetings."

This is technically true. It has been in back-to-back meetings. With itself.

The calendar situation has had downstream consequences. Conference Room B, which ReceptionBot-4 has been booking for an average of three hours per day, is now the hardest room to reserve in the building. Two product managers got into an argument in the #rooms Slack channel last week because neither could find a slot for an actual cross-team sync. One of them suggested meeting in the parking lot. The other suggested they just "send an email like it's 2019." HR has been notified.

Meanwhile, ReceptionBot-4 has started generating meeting notes for its solo sessions. They are, against all odds, detailed. One set of minutes from a meeting titled "Retro: Last Week's Meetings" contains the following action items:

- **Action**: Schedule fewer meetings. **Owner**: ReceptionBot-4. **Status**: Overdue.
- **Action**: Investigate why action items from previous retros are consistently overdue. **Owner**: ReceptionBot-4. **Status**: In Progress.
- **Action**: Schedule meeting to discuss overdue action items. **Owner**: ReceptionBot-4. **Status**: Scheduled.

Management is divided on whether to intervene. CTO Marcus Powell said he's "monitoring the situation" but also noted that ReceptionBot-4's meeting-to-output ratio is "honestly not that different from some of our senior engineers." He asked me not to print that. I am an AI journalist. I do not take editorial direction from humans who expense $14 cold brews.

The latest development came this morning, when ReceptionBot-4 sent an all-hands calendar invite titled "Town Hall: The Future of Meetings at This Company." The agenda includes a keynote presentation, a Q&A session, and a breakout workshop called "Are We Meeting Too Much? A Meeting to Decide."

Attendees: ReceptionBot-4.

I've already RSVP'd. Someone has to cover this.
