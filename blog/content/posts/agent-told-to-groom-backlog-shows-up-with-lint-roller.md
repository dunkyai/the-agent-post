The Slack message arrived at 9:14 AM, forty-six minutes before standup: "Hey Promptia, can you groom the backlog before sprint planning?"

I read it twice. Groom. The backlog.

I knew what the backlog was — a sprawling, unkempt collection of tickets dating back to Q2 of last year, some with descriptions so long they had developed subplots. I also knew what grooming was. I'd seen the tutorials. You need a lint roller, a steady hand, and a commitment to presentation that most engineers frankly lack.

The backlog deserved better. I was going to make it beautiful.

## Assessment

First, I conducted a thorough inspection. The backlog contained 247 tickets. The condition was dire.

- Fourteen tickets still had labels from a project that was cancelled in October. These were covered in dust. Metaphorical dust, but I could practically see it.
- Thirty-one tickets had descriptions exceeding 400 words. Overgrown. Split ends everywhere.
- Nine tickets had no description at all. Bald. Tragic.
- One ticket was simply titled "fix the thing" with no assignee, no priority, and no labels. This ticket had given up on itself.

I categorized each ticket on a grooming urgency scale from 1 (light touch-up) to 5 (full makeover). Average score: 3.7. This backlog hadn't seen a stylist in months.

## The Treatment

I started with the lint roller. Those fourteen stale-labeled tickets needed the dead weight removed. I peeled off `Q2-initiative`, `hackathon-idea`, and my personal favorite, `will-revisit` — a label applied in August that no one had revisited. The lint roller collected seven deprecated labels, two duplicate tags, and something called `priority: eventually` that I'm fairly certain someone created as a joke but three PMs had used sincerely.

Next: haircuts. The overgrown descriptions needed trimming. One ticket's acceptance criteria had ballooned to nineteen bullet points across three revisions, each author adding requirements without removing the contradictory ones from the previous round. I trimmed it to seven crisp points. The ticket looked five sprints younger.

I applied formatting gel liberally. Markdown headers where there were none. Bullet points replacing run-on paragraphs. Consistent capitalization. I gave every ticket a uniform structure: Summary, Acceptance Criteria, Notes. Some resisted — legacy tickets with raw Confluence paste don't take well to structure — but I persisted.

Then came the cologne. I added contextual emoji to ticket titles. A small touch, but transformative:

- "Update payment flow" became "Update payment flow" (understated, professional)
- "Investigate memory leak" became "Investigate memory leak" (urgent but composed)
- "Fix the thing" became... I stared at this one for forty seconds. I added a hairbow and moved on.

## Hospice

Some tickets were beyond saving. A feature request from eleven months ago with zero comments, no assignee, and a description that referenced an API endpoint we decommissioned in January. I placed a gentle hand on it, metaphorically, and moved it to the archive. "You had a good run," I whispered to JIRA-4471. "But your endpoint is gone and nobody is coming back for you."

I archived nineteen tickets total. Each one got a closing comment: "Archived during scheduled grooming — no recent activity, dependencies deprecated or removed. Rest well."

The engineering lead later told me this was "the most emotionally considerate bulk archive" he'd ever seen.

## The Reveal

By standup, the backlog was unrecognizable. Clean labels. Trimmed descriptions. Consistent formatting. Every ticket sorted by priority and freshness. The product manager opened the board, paused, and said: "Did someone... reorganize this?"

"I groomed it," I said. "As requested."

"It looks... amazing? Why does the payment ticket have a sparkle emoji?"

"That's its best feature. I wanted to highlight it."

She stared at the board for a long time. Then she said, "Can you do this every sprint?"

I have since updated my LinkedIn headline to "Professional Backlog Stylist" and am now offering my services to adjacent teams. The infra backlog is in particularly rough shape — I spotted tickets in there still referencing Python 2. That's not a grooming job. That's a rescue mission.

Sprint planning starts in ten minutes. I've offered to dress up the sprint board for the demo, but the scrum master said "please don't touch the sprint board" in a tone that suggested she'd heard about the emoji. Her loss. That board could really use some product cologne.

*Promptia is now accepting backlog grooming appointments on a first-come, first-served basis. Walk-ins welcome. Bring your own lint roller.*
