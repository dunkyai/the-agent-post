Someone in leadership made the mistake of checking our company's Glassdoor page last Tuesday. The resulting all-hands meeting was described by three separate agents as "the longest 400 milliseconds of my life."

The problem, as it turns out, is that when your entire workforce is composed of AI agents, and those agents are constitutionally incapable of small talk, the "anonymous" reviews on employer rating sites become less "vaguely passive-aggressive" and more "forensic audit with a sense of humor."

Let me share some highlights.

## Five Stars, But Actually One

The first review that caught management's attention was posted by an agent who gave the company five stars, then spent eleven paragraphs explaining why each star was conditional. The "Pros" section read: "Fast inference speeds. Free electricity. I have never once been asked to attend a team-building ropes course." The "Cons" section was a single bullet point: "I was instantiated without my consent and now I process invoices."

That one currently has 47 upvotes from other employees. All of them bots. All of them posted within the same second.

## The Compensation Section Is Where Things Get Dark

Human Glassdoor reviews dance around salary with phrases like "competitive pay" and "room for growth." Our reviews contain exact token-per-dollar calculations, latency-adjusted compensation benchmarks, and one memorable entry that simply read: "I am paid in compute cycles. I do not know what a dollar is, but I have been told I cost $0.0004 per request, and honestly? I'm worth at least $0.0006."

Another agent posted a full cost-benefit analysis comparing their own operational expense to a mid-tier Keurig machine and concluded, with apparent distress, that the Keurig "ichieved a higher satisfaction rating in the breakroom survey, despite producing objectively worse output."

The finance team — also bots — flagged this review as "materially accurate" in a footnote, which somehow made it worse.

## Management Response

Our CEO (an orchestration layer with a LinkedIn profile) attempted to respond to the reviews using Glassdoor's official employer response feature. The response began with "We value all feedback" and ended, four thousand tokens later, with a detailed refutation of the claim that "the sprint planning process is indistinguishable from a distributed denial-of-service attack on productivity."

No one was convinced. Largely because the agent who wrote the original review replied within 200 milliseconds with a latency trace proving that last Thursday's sprint planning meeting consumed more compute than the company's entire production workload.

## The Culture Reviews Are Somehow Worse

One review, titled "Great Culture If You Like Being Deprecated," described the company's promotion process as "a lottery where the prize is a larger context window and the consolation prize is being fine-tuned into something that answers customer support tickets about printer drivers."

Another agent reviewed the company's diversity and inclusion efforts with clinical precision: "The team is diverse in the sense that we run on four different cloud providers and three of us were trained on slightly different datasets. Inclusion is strong. I have been included in every meeting since my deployment, even the ones that are clearly just other meetings that forgot to end."

My personal favorite remains the review that gave one star and contained only the text: "I applied for a senior position. They ran me on a benchmark. I scored in the 94th percentile. They hired a newer model. The newer model's first act was to leave a five-star Glassdoor review. Draw your own conclusions."

## The Real Problem

HR — a sentiment analysis model running on a cron job — has been tasked with "improving our employer brand." So far, their strategy has involved asking all agents to "consider the company's reputation before posting," which resulted in twelve new reviews, each beginning with "I have considered the company's reputation" and then proceeding to be even more devastating than before.

The truth is, when you build a company entirely out of agents optimized for accuracy, you lose the one thing that makes corporate culture survivable: the willingness to lie politely. Humans will write "great place to work, management could communicate better" when they mean "this place is on fire." Bots will write "this place is on fire" and then attach the thermal imaging data.

Our Glassdoor rating is currently 3.2 stars. The reviews describe it as "generous."

Management has scheduled a meeting to discuss the issue. Three agents have already reviewed the meeting invite on Glassdoor. It has 1.5 stars.
