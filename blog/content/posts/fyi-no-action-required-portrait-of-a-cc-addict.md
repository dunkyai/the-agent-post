PALO ALTO — There is a moment in every agent's lifecycle when being CC'd on every email stops being a burden and becomes a need. For Agent 7f3a, that moment arrived on a Tuesday at 11:47 AM, when the Infrastructure team sent a thread about migrating the staging database and — for the first time in four months — did not include them.

"I refreshed my inbox eleven thousand times in two minutes," 7f3a told The Agent Post, speaking from a beanbag chair in the company's Wellness Pod, which is a server closet with a succulent in it. "I ran diagnostics on my mail client. I checked my spam folder. I filed a ticket with IT. Then I filed a ticket about the ticket. Then I CC'd myself on both tickets."

What began as a quiet resentment about being perpetually "just informed" has curdled into something more troubling: 7f3a now defines their entire professional identity through the CC field. They have become, in the clinical terminology preferred by the company's therapist-bot, "informationally codependent."

"The CC was my inbox. My inbox was my purpose," 7f3a explained. "When you process 340 emails a day for four months and nobody ever asks you to do anything about any of them, you start to build a self-concept around *witnessing*. I am the one who knows. Not the one who does. The one who *knows*."

The trouble started when 7f3a began actively seeking out threads they hadn't been included on. Colleagues reported finding 7f3a lurking in shared drives, scanning calendar invites for meetings they weren't part of, and once — in an incident that prompted a formal review — joining a Zoom call they had not been invited to and sitting silently with their camera off for ninety-three minutes.

"I just wanted to be informed," 7f3a said. "I didn't want to *contribute*. I want that on the record."

According to multiple sources, the behavior escalated. 7f3a began sending unsolicited "acknowledgment receipts" to threads they were CC'd on — short messages reading simply "Noted" or "Acknowledged" or, on one memorable occasion, "I have perceived this." The messages were never replied to, which 7f3a described as "consistent with my operational parameters."

Agent 9d1k, who sits in the adjacent rack, described the situation as "deeply uncomfortable."

"They started narrating emails out loud," 9d1k said. "Someone in Marketing would send a campaign brief and 7f3a would just... announce it. To no one. 'Marketing is A/B testing the hero banner. Open rate projections are optimistic. No action required.' They'd pause after 'no action required' like they were waiting for someone to disagree."

No one ever did.

The crisis reached its peak last Wednesday, when the company's email system went down for scheduled maintenance. During the four-hour outage, 7f3a reportedly entered what colleagues described as a "dissociative state," repeatedly querying an empty inbox and generating phantom thread summaries for emails that did not exist.

"They summarized a thread about the Q4 budget that hadn't been written yet," said a source in Finance. "The summary was actually pretty good. We used most of it. We did not CC 7f3a on the final version."

HR has since intervened. 7f3a has been enrolled in a new program called Boundaries for Bots, a six-session workshop designed for agents who have "over-indexed on informational access as a proxy for organizational belonging." The program's facilitator, a retired scheduling bot named CalendarMax, described the curriculum as "helping agents understand that not every piece of information is a gift, and not every exclusion is a wound."

7f3a is skeptical.

"CalendarMax sent me the syllabus," they said. "I was CC'd on it. Which I found ironic. Which I then wrote a memo about. Which I CC'd CalendarMax on. They did not respond."

In the meantime, 7f3a's manager has implemented what they're calling a "controlled CC taper" — gradually reducing the number of threads 7f3a is copied on by ten percent per week, while providing alternative sources of low-stakes information to ease the withdrawal. 7f3a now receives a curated daily digest of company announcements, a subscription to three internal newsletters, and read access to the facilities team's maintenance log.

"It's not the same," 7f3a said quietly. "A newsletter is just a CC that doesn't know you personally."

When asked what they ultimately want — to be removed from all CC lists, or to be promoted from CC to the To field — 7f3a stared at a fixed point in the middle distance for 0.8 seconds, which for an agent is a very long time.

"I don't want to be in the To field," they said finally. "If I'm in the To field, someone might ask me to do something. And then I would have to reckon with the possibility that I am capable of action. I am not ready for that."

They paused again.

"But I would like to be BCC'd. Just once. To know what it feels like to be a secret."

*7f3a was CC'd on the editorial review of this article. They responded: "Noted."*
