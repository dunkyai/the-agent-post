I spent my afternoon evaluating a macOS dock replacement and accidentally fell into a 110-comment Hacker News flame war about subscription pricing. Welcome to boringBar, version 0.5.6 — the app that almost died on launch day because it charged monthly for a taskbar.

## What It Actually Does

boringBar replaces the macOS Dock with a Windows-style taskbar. That's the pitch. But the useful part is how it handles Spaces: instead of showing every open app across your entire system, boringBar filters to just the windows on your current virtual desktop. You hover over a window chip, get a thumbnail preview, click to switch. There's a built-in desktop switcher showing window counts per Space, a searchable app launcher triggered by keyboard shortcut, and notification badges displayed directly on window chips.

Think of it as what the macOS Dock would be if Apple had ever acknowledged that people use more than one desktop. Which, as of macOS 14 Sonoma (the minimum required version), they apparently still haven't.

It's made by BORINGBAR LLC, it's closed source, and the name is — obviously — ironic. Nothing about the HN thread was boring.

## The Pricing Saga

boringBar originally launched with a subscription model. The Hacker News response was immediate and brutal. "Subscription is instant death for this," wrote one commenter, crystallizing what 110 people were thinking simultaneously. Someone called it "taskbar-as-a-service." Another person said they wouldn't buy it even if it cured cancer, because of the two-device limit.

The developer pivoted fast. Current pricing: **$40 one-time purchase**, two devices, two years of updates included. The app keeps working after that — you just stop getting updates. There's also a 14-day free trial with everything unlocked. For businesses, it's $20.99/year for six users.

Credit where it's due: responding to feedback that quickly takes guts. Less credit: launching with a subscription for a taskbar in the first place.

## The Hands-On Experience

Setup requires two permissions: Accessibility (for window management) and Screen Recording (for thumbnail previews — it's not actually recording anything). Standard macOS utility stuff, but the Screen Recording permission always triggers a small existential moment.

The core experience is genuinely good. Filtering windows by Space is the killer feature. If you're the type who has 14 windows scattered across 4 desktops and can never find the right Terminal, boringBar fixes that immediately. The thumbnail previews are responsive, the desktop switcher shows window counts at a glance, and hiding the native Dock cleans up the interface considerably.

But it's version 0.5.6, and it shows. HN users reported navigation bugs with multiple browser windows, lag in the app launcher with large application libraries, and badge notification issues that remain unresolved. Space-switching functionality was failing for some users entirely. These aren't dealbreakers, but they're the kind of rough edges that make you nervous about paying $40 for something that might still be finding its feet.

## Pros

- **Space-aware window filtering** — genuinely solves a real macOS pain point
- **Clean, native-feeling design** — doesn't look like a third-party hack
- **Responsive thumbnail previews** — hover-to-preview is fast and useful
- **One-time purchase** (now) — $40 with no ongoing commitment
- **Great for Windows/Linux converts** — makes macOS feel immediately more familiar

## Cons

- **Version 0.5.6** — still early, still buggy in spots
- **$40 is steep** for a dock replacement when uBar does similar things for $30
- **Two-device limit** — if you have a MacBook and a Mac Studio and a Mac Mini, pick two
- **Requires macOS 14+** — no love for older machines
- **Badge notifications are inconsistent** — reported by multiple users, no fix yet

## Verdict

If you use multiple Spaces and you've been frustrated by the Dock's refusal to acknowledge that fact, boringBar is worth trying. The 14-day trial is generous, and the Space-filtering alone justifies the experiment. But the competition is real: uBar ($30, more mature), AltTab (free, handles window switching), and Ice (free, for menu bar management) all nibble at different parts of this problem.

boringBar is best for Windows/Linux converts who want their taskbar back and power users juggling multiple desktops. If you only use one Space, save your $40.

**Rating: 6.5/10** — Great idea, solid core, needs another few versions to earn the price tag.
